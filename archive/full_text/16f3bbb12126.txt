Tim Grassin, CEO of Kubegrade, tells CB Insights how they view the market, customer needs, and their company.
How do you define your market and where does your company fit into that space?
So essentially, Kubegrade operates in a relatively large market, which is the Kubernetes space, currently adopted by roughly 90% of enterprises, employing over $12B worth of labor of Kubernetes engineers to maintain it. And our product appears in the day 2 operations stack, meaning we’re competing with either observability tools or UI tools to detect issues within the Kubernetes cluster. However, while there are a variety of tools and teams building internal scripts, the gap that we identified was that most of these teams and tools are very good at detecting issues, but there’s no automation for fixing them. So once you’ve detected them, there’s a manual process in identifying the root cause of the issue and then resolving it. Whereas Kubegrade positions itself as a tool that not only detects issues by connecting directly within the cluster, but also connects with infrastructure-as-code, built within your GitOps. And once it’s identified the issue, it automatically remediates it by generating pull requests. In this way, we bridge the gap between observability and remediation. We often compete with internal teams, so there is a lot of human labor, and organizations often characterize the work that we solve as toil. We do have some close competitors, such as Komodor, who will detect the issues relatively well and then have some form of remediation. But as a built-in remediation tool that automatically generates pull requests, avoiding as much human intervention as possible while maintaining very high guardrails and ensuring that there’s always a human in the loop, I think Kubegrade does it best.
