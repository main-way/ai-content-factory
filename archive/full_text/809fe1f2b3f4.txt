AI is wildly disrupting the world at large, and tech more specifically, and one manifestation of that is the advent of new devices like smart glasses. I've been covering the emergence of wearables, virtual reality and augmented reality for over 15 years, but the current moment is more in flux than any I've witnessed.
This week, I'm in Long Beach, California, for AWE 2026, the biggest AR/VR-focused conference of the year. I'm seeing and reporting on what's new and how AI is taking up residence in what a lot of companies hope will be everyday eyewear for all of us.
Meta already has tons of smart glasses, Google is releasing a wave of its own later this year, and Snap, a maker of smart glasses for years before Meta, on Tuesday introduced new augmented reality Specs that are coming out in the fall. Apple's new infusion of Siri AI into all its devices, announced last week at WWDC -- where I got an in-depth look at how Siri works -- hints at smart glasses that could arrive next year. It's a lot to keep track of.
VR's still got things going on, too. Apple is evolving its Vision Pro software, Bytedance-owned Pico has a high-end mixed-reality headset on its way, and Valve's standalone Steam Frame should arrive this summer. Google and Xreal are pushing into new territory with the VR-like, glasses-sized Project Aura, also expected this year.
At AWE, I'm asking questions and exploring what's happening while Google, Meta and Apple keep advancing the technology, and peeking around the corner at chips, displays and sensors (and experiences) that could show us where wearables are heading next.

The on-device AI capabilities of Qualcomm's new Snapdragon Reality Elite chip, debuting on Google's Android XR-powered Xreal Aura glasses this fall, could do some things we haven't seen on VR headsets before. A demo of a Gaussian splat 3D scan of a person turned into a talking-head AI avatar, all run on the Aura's processing puck, was demoed at Qualcomm's AWE booth.

Neural bands with heart rate? Mudra's research platform shows a path to new wearables
By
Scott Stein
Meta's neural band, a wrist-worn neural input-sensing band used to control its latest smart glasses, could be a doorway to more. EMG, or electromyography, the tech it employs, could be used for more gestures, more interfaces, and maybe even doorways to health tech.
Wearable Devices, maker of the Mudra Link neural band, has been exploring EMG tech for years. It showed me its Mudra Pro wearable for research, a PPG optical heart rate-plus-four-sensor EMG band that could be used to explore connections between health and neural impulse data. The plans are to include more metal EMG sensors (eight) in the next version, wrapping around the wrist, plus an EDA sensor (electrodermal activity, which registers sweat levels).
No one's put neural tech into a smartwatch yet, although there's a Mudra band that doubles as an Apple Watch strap. Meta's reported to have a smartwatch in the works, and it could fold the tech in.
I also checked out Mudra Studio, a new playground for customizing neural band inputs, and came away wowed. The computer platform can let the wristband's neural signals be customized and coded into games or training apps or anything else, registering new gestures or even recognizing intentions to move (like clicking a mouse) before the mouse is even clicked.

With concerns growing over privacy and personal control in AI and wearable tech, it's fascinating to see a pair of glasses promising a Linux-based operating system and hackable expandability that's also privacy-conscious. The Raven Prism, coming later this year, is exactly that pair of glasses.

I tried a VR mixed reality headset as small as a pair of smart glasses
By
Scott Stein
VR and AR glasses are in the process of merging, sort of: Xreal's Aura is a prime example. The URXR One, by Unseen Realities, coming later this year, is another. The startup's hardware is shockingly compact, but they're absolutely VR with pass-through mixed reality cameras, not actual see-through glasses. They're not standalone, either: They plug into a nearby computer to extend displays and run apps.
I wore them connected to a laptop to see how the display felt. With pass-through, I felt like the laptop display was large and hovering over the room. It was similar to the mixed-reality effect you'd get on a Vision Pro or Galaxy XR, with slightly less impressive resolution (2,448x2,064 pixels per eye, and micro OLED, 90Hz). The hardware uses pancake lenses for clearer optics, and it has built-in 6DoF (six degrees of freedom) room tracking.
The 90-degree diagonal field of view is VR standard, but it feels bigger because my peripheral vision bleeds over from the edges of the glasses, making the pass-through feel extended. It's a clever illusion, reminding me of what I liked about the Meta Quest Pro way back.
And they only weigh 93 grams, 23 grams more than Meta's Ray-Ban Displays. I'm curious to try them again.

A road trip to LA for a sci-fi mixed reality theater funeral

Scott Stein
AWE is kicking off today, and last night at 11 p.m. I went up to Hollywood from Long Beach to see the LA Fringe premiere of ROOT (Running Out of Time), a mixed reality play that was a funeral service, taking place in the future, for a person who extended their life digitally into mixed reality.
The play, written and directed by Kristen Gorlitz and featuring real actors, also has a live human-piloted AR avatar that appears on audience phones during the show. Much like in Escape the Internet, but to much different effect, audience members connected to a private Wi-Fi node and server using a QR code to be part of the experience.
ROOT explores life extension, class divides and a protest against digital life extension that starts outside the theater and can be heard at points during the show. I thought about the storytelling possibilities, the challenges, and the questions I walked away with.
And, what will glasses and headsets do to transform ideas like this? Could they help or hinder? I saw a mixed reality play called An Ark in New York earlier this year, a performance that was totally virtual in a real space, seen on Magic Leap 2 glasses. It was also funereal. But in the real world, no one has or can afford AR glasses. Will we find solutions for art that uses phones or bypass tech in favor of other solutions? Or are phones still a sign of more connected wearable tech blending with theater to come?

Before AWE, a trip to Tribeca to explore the questions of AI and us
By
Scott Stein
What better way to kick off a whole show about Al's increasing advances on the future of glasses, augmented reality and virtual reality than a trip to revisit an interactive game played in a movie theater that questions our dependence on algorithms? Escape the Internet (Part 1), a brilliant experimental work by longtime AR/VR pioneer Lucas Rizzotto, was something I tried at SXSW in Austin earlier this year. It returned in a new iteration at Tribeca Film Festival last week in a totally new form, and I played it... and talked to Lucas and Technical Director Pete Cybriwsky afterward.
The interactive game (and performance) uses phones to connect to a private server that tracks answers to various survey questions, highlights who shared what, and has interactive games, like shooting arrows at characters on-screen to vote for moral and personal decisions. In the new version, some audience members even went onstage at the front of the theater to share their stories and drive vehicles in the projected games, made in Unity, using a connected steering wheel rig.
Rizzotto sees this ongoing game as a way to connect real people in ways that VR and AR couldn't, and demonstrates how real expressions between people can topple the tyranny of algorithms. The new version was even warmer, more inspiring and more funny.
Are there any answers for how to escape the internet? Will a show about AI, smart glasses and augmented reality provide any answers? The questions are building. And art is part of the way forward.
Smart glasses are becoming mainstream
By
Jon Skillings
It's getting more and more likely that you or someone you pass by on the street is wearing smart glasses, or might at least try them out in the near future. Market researcher IDC is predicting that "2026 is the year smart glasses graduate from early adopter curiosity to a mainstream force."
The company said Monday that sales surged 167% year over year in the first quarter of 2026, with 2.25 million units shipped during that three-month period.
There's a clear leader of the pack: Meta, whose Ray-Ban smart glasses lineup accounted for 69% of the market in the first quarter. The most anyone else could muster individually was a 3.4% share; that was RayNeo, followed by Xiaomi (3.1%), Viture (2.5%) and Xreal (2%).
But how long will Meta remain top dog? Jitesh Ubrani, an IDC research manager tracking mobile devices, wrote in the Smart Glasses Surge report that "the challengers assembling against it are formidable."
He pointed to two companies in particular: Google and Snap. Google, he writes, is getting into the smart glasses fray with decided advantages with its Android XR ecosystem and its presence in people's email, photos, search history and calendars. "When someone puts on a pair of Android XR glasses, the AI assistant doesn't need an introduction," Ubrani wrote. "It already knows you."
Snap, meanwhile, "has tens of thousands of developers who have spent years building AR experiences," plus "real-world learnings on optics, thermal management, and social comfort that simply cannot be shortcut."
If you find yourself tempted by smart glasses this year, no matter the manufacturer, some credit will have to go to Meta. "The Ray-Ban Meta lineup has done something rare in consumer tech," Ubrani wrote. "It created a device people are genuinely unafraid to be seen wearing in public."
